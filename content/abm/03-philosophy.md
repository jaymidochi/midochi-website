### A story about two ABM programs

Two companies launched ABM programs in the same vertical: Europe banks, average deal size around $100K.

Team 1 had every advantage on paper. A sales-led org that had already grown through outbound. 5 reps plus a full marketing team. A $200K budget for an ABM platform and program. A well-known agency on retainer.

Team 2 had almost nothing. A marketer new to the role. One rep with a thin read on the customer's challenges and no background in the space. No ABM stack. $10K.

Here's what each of them did.

Team 1 went broad. One industry, thousands of accounts, same message to all of them. A gated report as the "warm-up." Automated outreach with a bit of personalization sprinkled on top. The whole program assumed buyers move in a straight line: aware, interested, MQL, SQL, closed.

Team 2 went narrow. They clustered accounts based on use case and then prioritized accounts by how likely each one was to become a deal. Built playbooks around how their buyers move through a decision. Tied KPIs to the activities that drive that movement.

Team 2 won.

$10K and no platform meant they couldn't afford to be generic. That constraint did more work than Team 1's budget ever did.

### Why this keeps happening

Almost every time, the root cause looks like Team 1. Upload a wish list of accounts to a platform. Run programmatic ads to a gated PDF.

Track clicks as "engagement." Hand the warm ones to sales as MQLs. Add everyone else to a cadence. Repeat next quarter.

That's lead generation with an ABM sticker on it. Same volume mindset, same straight-line assumptions, bigger logos.

### What we actually believe

**A cluster beats a vertical.** "Banks" is too broad to write to in one voice.

A cluster is a specific group of accounts dealing with the same problem. Narrow enough that one message and one playbook genuinely fit all of them.

Most programs that feel generic were targeted by vertical and tier, with no cluster ever defined.

**Deal probability beats logo size.** A list of "companies we'd love to land" is a wish list.

We score accounts on engagement signals, evidence of need, existing relationships, and business triggers. Then we focus where the probability is actually highest.

**Buyer journeys beat funnels.** Complex B2B deals can take 1000+ touchpoints to close.

Most of those happen where your CRM can't see them: peer conversations, internal meetings, content nobody clicked a tracked link to find.

**Evolution beats revolution.** You already run webinars, content, outreach.

ABM means pointing those at the right accounts with the right message. That's it. No new program bolted onto your existing silos.

**One team, one account list.** Marketing and sales working the same accounts, with shared visibility and the same definition of progress.

These five ideas shape everything below.
